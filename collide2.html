Collide - Notifications

Serve users notifications that are actionable and/or informative.

Case study

As a user, I want to receive notifications so that I can stay updated on my account's activity.

Understanding the intent

Notifications are implemented to alert users of activity related to their account. They can be beneficial and irritating, thus must be curated with explicit intent and elegance. Users allowing notifications from a product is an act of trust, and we do not want to take that for granted.

Good notifications are:

  • Non-interfering - Let users know that something important has happened and would be beneficial for the user to pay attention to.
  • Contextual - Notifications should be direct. Be explicit in letting the user know what has happened and whether they should actively interact with it or not.
  • Manageable - Give the users the ability to manage how and when these notifications occur.*

Bad notifications are:

  • Valueless - If there is no direct value for the user to interact with the notice, then it should not be a notification.
  • Disruptive - Do not cause a false sense of urgency. Notifications should be served when the user is not in the progress of an activity.
  • Generic - Unless necessary, information within notifications shouldn't be generic.

"Smart notifications will feel more like a note from your assistant or another human being"

Intercom

Problem

Users weren't alerted of new activity to their account. Collide notifications are currently being served through direct SMS (twilio) and emails. These are problematic for many reasons. Twilio SMS notifications are costly and unsoliciated. They also enforces the user to an experience outside of Collide. As far as emails go, our metrics show most of our emails don't end up being opened.

Proposed solution

Implement in-app notifications. It's no surprise a notification system is beneficial to a product and its users. What is surprising is how Collide has gone this long without one. This was one of my biggest goals to achieve during my time at Collide.

What makes sense for Collide

By understanding the basic principles of notifications, Mixed Model notifications works best for Collide. Collide's business model poses the need for a complex and diverse suite of features to fit business needs. Mixed Model notifications will easily scale with the product as it continues to grow.

Let's break down how notifications will be built. Mixed Model notifications consist of a Source and an Anchor. One determines where the notification is coming from, while the other determines where it should be sent.

Sources determine...

  • Profile - Relating to the profile of a user. Change if bio, new followers/subscribers, etc..
  • Posts - Relating to activity of a specific post. Comments, Likes, etc..
  • Events - Relating to primary functional activity between a Supporter and Creator. New Uploads, new Subscriptions, etc..
  • Connect - Relating to any activity of the in-app messenger, video chat, and phone calls.

Anchors are...

  • Messenger - All related activity around our in-app messenger will be directed directly to its own anchor.
  • Notification Center - This interface will house most notifications that is served to users. Each notification will be distinguishable by the type of source

Implementation

There are two main things to focus on; creating the notifications and anchors, and getting user permission to subscribe to receive notifications.

  • Implement a custom banner that asks users to enable notifications. They can either allow (which will prompt browser permission prompt) or dismiss it.
  • Ask users to allow notifications when the benefits are most obvious to them. (i.e. when a supporter requests a video chat from a creator, serve a message that says "Would you like to be notified when your video chat is ready?").

Final thoughts

There are several things to be concerned about the solution we have picked. One would be users not allowing permission for Collide to send them notifications. Which will defeat the whole purpose of notifications.

Another problem is not implementing a way for users to manage their notifications. This is a temporary hold for now and will be implemented once resources are available.

Overall, finally having a notifications will bring lots of positivities to Collide that will ultimately drive user success and engagement.
